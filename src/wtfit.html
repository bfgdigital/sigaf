What The F*ck Am I Looking At?

The Philosophy: No Charts, No Interpretation

The Bitcoin protocol doesn't care about tweets, regulations, or media hype. Its forward march is relentless. The USD price is just a noisy, human interpretation of this progress. There are other metrics that would be great to use like the difficulty adjustments for eg, but, at the end of the day, people care about price.

My beef is that standard charts are filled with patterns that demand interpretation, a process that is often subjective and error-prone. As a data guy, I look at a lot of charts and have come to realise you can bin 99% of them, you're probably not helping yourself by trying to interpret them.

This tool is an attempt to filter that noise. It's not a tool for finding entry and exit points; it's a "dashboard" for a quick, objective reading of market momentum. It asks one question: "Is the protocol's momentum, as measured by price, holding up relative to its entire history?"

The Methodology: Make a box and measure angles

To provide an objective answer, we have to escape the distortions of absolute price and time. This model does so by converting Bitcoin's entire history into a single, normalized 1x1 geometric space.

  1. How does this help? Bitcoin's history is fit into a 1x1 square. The x-axis is its age in blocks (from 0 to the current block height), and the y-axis is its price relative to the All-Time High (from $0 to $ATH). In this space, a price movement from 2013 can be directly compared to one from last week.
  2. The Macro Trend Angle: We calculate the angle formed by a straight line from the origin to the current price and block. Our baseline (best-case scenario) is a 45º diagonal line from the origin that passes through the all-time high (ATH). The deviation of our current trend angle from this 45º ideal is a key input that tells the model about the health of the overall macro trend.
  3. The Micro Trend Horizons: To measure momentum changes, we look backward from the current price to dynamically chosen points we call "horizons." Unlike a fixed timeframe (e.g., "90 days"), these horizons are adaptively generated based on the market's current character, ensuring we are always asking the most relevant questions.

The Micro Measurement: A "Momentum of Momentum" Analysis

The final verdict is a weighted consensus that measures the market's acceleration or deceleration across different timeframes.

1. Deriving Adaptive Horizons

The analysis horizons are not fixed; they are generated using a method inspired by signal processing to adapt to market conditions:

  • To identify the key “horizons” to measure price behavior, the model uses a "chirp" signal. The peaks of this wave give us non-linear points in Bitcoin’s history to look back on.
  • The shape of that chirp is calibrated by blending two macro signals: the **Quadratic Curvature** (is the trend accelerating?) and the **Macro Angular Ratio** (is the trend healthy?).
  • If the macro trend is strong and decisive, the peaks are further apart, causing the model to sample a **broad range of the past** (e.g., 3 weeks to 6 months) to understand the context of the large move. If macro momentum is slowing, the peaks cluster toward the present, giving us a **tighter lens** on recent volatility (e.g., 10 days to 80 days). This makes the model adapt to the market’s tempo, not a rigid clock.

2. Calculating Corrected Momentum Deviation

This is the core of the analysis. Instead of just comparing a micro trend to the overall macro trend, we compare each timeframe's momentum to the next longest one in a sequential chain. This measures the *rate of change* of momentum.

Crucially, this comparison is **corrected for the natural geometric distortion** that occurs when you "zoom in" on a trend line. 

// Simplified Concept
Expected_Angle_S = Actual_Angle_L * (Lookback_S / Lookback_L)
Deviation = Actual_Angle_S - Expected_Angle_S

This process isolates the *true* acceleration or deceleration, giving a pure signal of whether momentum is building or fading relative to the established trend of the prior period. The status for each horizon (Peachy, No Bueno, etc.) is then assigned based on how significant this deviation is relative to the others.

  • PARTY: Strong, unexpected positive acceleration ($Δ > 1.5σ$).
  • PEACHY: Mild positive acceleration ($0.5σ < Δ \le 1.5σ$).
  • MEH: Momentum is behaving as expected ($-0.5σ \le Δ \le 0.5σ$).
  • WATCH: Mild negative acceleration (deceleration) ($-1.5σ \le Δ < -0.5σ$).
  • NO BUENO: Strong deceleration or trend breakdown ($Δ < -1.5σ$).

3. Reaching a Weighted Consensus

Each horizon casts a "vote" based on its status. However, these votes are not all equal. The consensus is **weighted to give more influence to longer-term signals**. This reflects a core philosophy: in a strong trend, the established, longer-term momentum is a more reliable signal than short-term, noisy volatility. This prevents a single bad day from overriding months of positive momentum, leading to a more robust and stable final verdict.

Visualizing The Geometry

While this tool avoids charts for its final analysis, visualizing the underlying geometry can help overcome my poor explanations. The chart page demonstrates a core problem with subjective analysis: perspective changes everything.

You will see the macro trend line, which looks steep from a distance, appear nearly flat when you zoom in on the present moment. This distortion is precisely why this meter was built—to replace subjective interpretation with objective calculation.
